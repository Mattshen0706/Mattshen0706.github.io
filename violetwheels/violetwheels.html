New York City thrives on its massive transportation network, moving millions across its boroughs daily. As part of PlaNYC’s push to improve quality of life and cut carbon emissions, the city has prioritized investments in public transit, walking, and biking. But despite these efforts, biking remains the least utilized option, with only 2.6% of commuters choosing it (PlaNYC). With 472 subway stations and over 3 million daily riders (MTA), the subway is the backbone of NYC’s commute. While it’s a step in the right direction for energy efficiency, there’s still huge potential for an even greener system, one that better integrates bikes and makes use of existing bike infrastructure. However, launching a citywide campaign to promote biking comes with challenges. Mayor Adams once explored subsidizing CitiBike in 2021, but budget constraints kept it from moving forward (Brachfeld). Rather than tackling NYC as a whole, a more practical starting point would be focusing on a specific community such as NYU. By working with NYU and CitiBike, we can work to introduce a discounted or complimentary CitiBike membership for students, making biking a more convenient and affordable option. If successful, this could serve as a model for expanding bike access across the city, making sustainable transportation a more realistic choice for more New Yorkers.

Since the 1800s, when biking was first introduced in New York City, biking culture has undergone intense changes. At first, there were concerns that biking put everyone's lives at risk, so in 1880, officials tried to outlaw bikes from the city’s parks for the safety of everyone. Bikes were still used, however, to run daily errands and to feel a sense of freedom, specifically for women, minorities, and immigrants (Mcshane). Bikes stood as a symbol for these groups, since they provided a sense of belonging in a huge city. This fear of biking lasted throughout the years, however, causing countless disagreements between politicians and residents, like in 1980 when cyclists had a sit-in on the Avenue of Americas to protest the removal of the bike lanes (Mcshane). As a result, actions were taken to find a middle ground that would let bikes coexist peacefully. In 1938, one of the first bike paths was introduced in Queens on the Northern State Parkway (Sixthreezero). In 2007, NYC added its first protected bike lane on 9th avenue (Mcshane). These two events on their own started the domino effect of building bike paths throughout the city. The history of biking is important in demonstrating how since change has been successful in the past, future change is possible, which is what we are asking you to help with today.
Change is possible; as of now, 1,240+ miles of bike lanes have been installed and more than 480 miles of these lanes are protected. Also, bike paths in parks have also been on the rise. This increase in bike lanes, as well as the introduction of bike sharing, has dramatically increased users. From 2012-2017, there was a 26% growth of bike riders, which is about 175,000 people, and the average annual growth of daily cycling was 9.1% (Cycling in the City). This increase is thanks to developments in the city’s infrastructure, accessibility, safety, and awareness. Nearly 1.9 million New Yorkers ride bikes, so streets need to be adapted to safely fit cars, bikes, and pedestrians (Bikes and Pedestrians). In September 2021, a new bike lane was introduced on the Brooklyn Bridge to allow for biking that did not intervene with other modes of transportation. Biking is one of the most effective and accessible ways to combat climate change, especially in densely populated cities like New York. Replacing even a single car trip with a bike ride can drastically cut down an individual’s carbon footprint by nearly 67% (How Riding). Cars emit nearly five metric tons of CO₂ per year (How Riding), and switching to bikes, which produce only about 21 grams of CO₂ per kilometer compared to 271 grams for cars, can significantly reduce the greenhouse gases contributing to global warming (Bike Portland). In fact, if more New Yorkers opted to bike for short errands or commutes, transportation-related emissions, one of the top contributors to climate change would greatly decrease (How Riding). The environmental benefits of biking extend beyond just CO₂ reductions. Biking instead of driving helps eliminate harmful chemicals, particulates, and nitrogen oxides from the air, which improves overall air quality and can reduce rates of respiratory illness and disease (How Riding). Programs like Citi Bike have already made a meaningful impact, cutting about 900 tons of CO₂ each month and saving over 30,000 tonnes of emissions over a four-year period (The Science Survey; Discovery UCL). On top of that, biking contributes to quieter, less congested neighborhoods. Unlike cars or even subways, which contribute to noise pollution, bikes offer a near-silent alternative, promoting a more peaceful and livable urban environment. By incorporating cycling into everyday routines, even if its just once a week, individuals can help eliminate millions of tons of carbon emissions annually (How Riding), paving the way for a cleaner, quieter, and healthier city.

One promising initiative NYU could adopt to promote sustainable transportation is launching its own bike rental program exclusively for students, faculty, and staff. With a simple ID-based check-out system, members of the NYU community could borrow a bike from designated campus hubs and return it after use, ensuring accessibility and accountability.
